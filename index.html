Project-Based Learning: A Project Management Approach

üìò CHAPTER 1: INTRODUCTION

The handbook explores project-based learning (PBL) from a project management (PM) perspective, arguing that successful PBL requires commitment to both student learning and well-structured, well-managed project execution. It highlights that many elements of PBL, especially the phases, are adapted from professional PM practices.

The author directly addresses the following professional PM knowledge areas with students:

  • Scope Management: Delineating project requirements.
  • Human Resource Management: Leading or working with a project team.
  • Stakeholder Management: Working with a project client or sponsor.
  • Communications Management: Keeping everyone informed.
  • Time Management: Managing time and/or creating schedules.
  • Risk Management: Planning for and managing project risks.
  • Quality Management: Ensuring the project leads to quality outcomes.

The internationally recognized standards for professional project management are maintained by the Project Management Institute (PMI) in A Guide to the Project Management Body of Knowledge (PMBOK¬Æ Guide).

üìò CHAPTER 2: PROJECT-BASED LEARNING

This chapter introduces and defines PBL and outlines its seven phases.

‚ú® Defining Project-Based Learning

PBL is a collaborative inquiry teaching approach where students work in teams on a curriculum-based project with real-world connections, guided by a driving question.

The definition is: Project-based learning empowers learners to collaborate in teams, mentored by their instructors, as they research real-world questions, pose solutions to real-world problems, and design real-world products in a rigorous way.

  • PBL as an 'Instructional Umbrella': It's not a panacea but can serve as a framework for employing a wide range of instructional strategies.
  • Key components: It empowers students by shifting control; reconceptualizes students as learners; emphasizes collaboration in teams; requires instructors to act as mentors/facilitators; focuses on real-world questions and problems; and demands a rigorous process tied to curriculum standards.
‚è≥ Project Phases (The 7 Phases)

Seven project phases, adapted from PM, scaffold the learning:

  1. Preparing: Structuring lessons, activities, and discussions to teach students pre-requisite content and PM skills (e.g., group development, communication, roles, scheduling, best practices).
  2. Initiating: Summarizing a feasible project idea, defining the scope, and securing authorization to proceed (often via a mini-proposal).
  3. Planning: Detailing the project plans and securing stakeholder support, resulting in a formal Project Proposal (the project plan).
  4. Executing and 5. Assessing (in tandem): Carrying out the project while continuously monitoring its progress to ensure success. Project teams track, report, and assess progress against the plan.
    • The Triple-M Cycle (Manage, Monitor, Modify) is a developed process for continuous monitoring: Tracking (documentation), Reporting (communication), and Assessing (evaluation, leading to modification).
  5. Publishing: Disseminating the final product(s) to a public audience, underscoring the real-world focus and increasing the stakes for students.
  6. Closing: Bringing the project to a structured end, including securing sign-off from the sponsor (if applicable), archiving project assets, and documenting best practices and lessons learned through reflection.
üìò CHAPTER 3: THE PROJECT IMPETUS

This chapter focuses on the motivation behind a project and the critical early stages of project initiation and definition.

üë§ Project Initiation & Locus of Control

A project can be initiated by the instructor, students, or an external sponsor. The locus of control is often related to who initiates the project:

  • Instructor-Initiated: Instructor retains a high degree of control, ideal for training students in PBL or tightly aligning with curriculum.
  • Student-Initiated: Students invest in and control their own learning, leveraging curiosity, supporting metacognitive goals, and aligning the topic with team interests/expertise.
  • Sponsor-Initiated: Deepens real-world authenticity by partnering with external clients; increases the stakes for students to meet sponsor expectations.
‚ùì The Rise of the Problematic Situation

The most compelling projects are often driven by the need to solve or address a social or scientific problem, described as a problematic situation (a concept from John Dewey). This is a disequilibrium that triggers a need for new learning and propels student inquiry throughout the project lifecycle.

üéØ From Inspiration to Driving Question

Broad project ideas must be honed into a focused topic to manage scope and provide clear direction, preventing: broad but shallow learning, tangents, and insufficient time.

The project is then propelled by a Driving Question‚Äîa carefully crafted, open-ended question that captures the project's goal.

‚úÖ Qualities of Effective Driving Questions

Ideally, a driving question should be:

  • Authentic: Drawn from controversies central to a discipline/profession.
  • Challenging: Requires students to grow their learning and skills.
  • Clear: Avoids vagueness.
  • Controversial: Provokes multiple perspectives.
  • Empowering: Motivates students to make a difference.
  • Feasible: Not unduly overwhelming.
  • Local/Real-world based: Directly applicable to the 'real world'.
  • Motivating: Sustains student interest.
  • Multi-disciplinary: Requires drawing on multiple subject areas.
  • Open-ended: Cannot be answered with a simple "yes/no" or quick search.
  • Standards-based: Directly addresses curriculum standards/learning outcomes.
  • Timely: Addresses current issues.
üìù The Mini Proposal

For projects requiring considerable planning, a mini proposal is drafted first. It helps determine a project's feasibility, potential risks, and alignment before investing in a full proposal. It is roughly equivalent to a professional project charter and delineates:

  • Project Title, Supervising Instructor, Subject Area(s), Audience, Sponsor/Client.
  • Project Summary and Driving Question.
  • Alignment with curriculum standards/learning outcomes.
  • A preliminary list of final product(s) and project team members.
üìò CHAPTER 4: THE PROJECT PROPOSAL

The Project Proposal is the formal planning document‚Äîexpanding on the mini proposal‚Äîthat guides the project team's work during the executing phase.

  • Rolling Wave Planning is noted as a professional PM strategy that allows iterative planning to continue throughout the executing phase for lengthy or complex projects.
üìã Proposal Components

Key sections of a full project proposal (which may vary depending on the project):

  • Title: Formal, compelling name for the project.
  • Duration: Anticipated start and completion dates.
  • Audience: The intended public audience for the final products (beyond classmates/instructor), categorized from Level 1 (Classmates) to Level 4 (Competition).
  • Sponsor/Client: The outside organization or client commissioning/partnering on the project (if applicable).
  • Summary: A brief (e.g., 100-150 words) overview of the project.
  • Driving Question: The focused, open-ended question driving student learning.
  • Rationale: The reason for the project, addressing the social/scientific problem and the benefits to be derived.
  • Alignment with Curriculum Standards/Learning Outcomes: Specific standards/outcomes the project will address, ideally across multiple subject areas.
  • Content and Skills: A concise bullet list of specific knowledge and skills to be addressed (grouped into subject content, technical skills, habits of mind, and project management skills).
  • Deliverables: The formal term for the tangible products (Final Products and Interim Deliverables).
    • Final Deliverables communicate research, pose solutions, or produce a real-world product (e.g., report, prototype, map, video game).
    • Interim Deliverables include drafts/prototypes and all tracking/reporting documents (calendars, Gantt charts, status reports).
  • Personnel: Student team members, their respective roles (Casting Students into Role as real-world professionals), and outside experts/advisors.
  • Resources: Facilities, equipment, tools, and materials needed (beyond typical classroom staples).
  • Budget: Cost of equipment, resources, and other expenses, and revenue generation plans (if applicable).
  • Tasks: Discrete units of work needed for completion, determined through Task Delineation (breaking the project down, often using a Work Breakdown Structure - WBS) and task scheduling.
    • Project Milestones demarcate key culminating points (e.g., status report, full team meeting, interim assessment).
  • Schedule: The timeline for work, detailing the sequence, effort, and start/end dates/times for each task (often using a Gantt chart).
  • Approval and Reporting Requirements: How often progress is reported to the instructor and where explicit approval is needed to move to the next stage.
  • Risks and Constraints: Potential risks and mitigation strategies, and any constraints the project must work within.
  • Assessment Rubrics: The rubrics to be used for assessing final and interim deliverables, often attached as appendices.
  • Contract Agreements: Formal agreements negotiated with outside providers, referenced in the proposal and attached as appendices.
üîí Approving the Project Proposal

The proposal draft is strengthened through ongoing formative assessment feedback from the instructor and, optionally, a panel of peers.

  • Assessing the Proposal Draft: The instructor/review panel may request changes ranging from scaling back the scope to refining the driving question, strengthening the rationale, or detailing tasks/schedule.
  • Locus of Control for Approval: Can be Instructor-centred (sole sign-off) or Student-centred (joint feedback from instructor and student peers).
üó£Ô∏è The Proposal Review Panel

A project team benefits from verbally pitching its project plans (a 5- to 10-minute pitch) to the instructor and a panel of peers, which tightens their understanding and enhances oral literacy. The panel assesses the proposal based on criteria like likelihood for success, specificity, realistic scope/timeframes, originality, and contribution to societal/scientific need. A successful pitch results in the formal approval of the project proposal, which becomes the project plan.

This chapter lists resources developed by the author and available for download, with the intent of sharing for non-commercial educational purposes.

  • EduProject.org: A knowledge mobilization portal for PBL, featuring monographs, professional development links, open-access research, videos, and a bookstore.
  • Project Management Course Outline: For the author's course IASC 2P01: Foundations of Project Management (Brock University), which draws on students' own project experiences across diverse contexts. It aligns to seven PM knowledge areas (scope, human resource, stakeholder, communications, time, risk, quality).
  • Proposal Brief Template: A template used as the culminating assignment in the IASC 2P01 course.
  • Project Management Inventory: A tool to help students identify their PM strengths and learning goals, focusing on communications, human resource, risk, and scope management.
  • PBL/PM Conceptual Model: A visual model that scaffolds projects for students in the Brock University Design Studio (BUDS), showing the flow of interactions between the student team and an external project sponsor throughout the project lifecycle.
  • Exemplars: Examples of project management organizers developed by students, including a Statement of Work (concise summary of specific project requirements) and a Work Breakdown Structure (WBS) (hierarchical diagram of atomized tasks).
